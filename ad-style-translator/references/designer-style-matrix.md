# Designer Style Matrix

This matrix is derived from the user's document `十位平面设计师风格分析_2026-06-13.docx`. Use it as a practical style-routing reference for advertising posters, packaging visuals, KVs, and print campaign prompts.

Do not paste designer names into the final image-generation prompt as style commands. Use the names for route selection, then translate into visual DNA.

## 1. Paula Scher

One-line route: Typographic jazz; bold uppercase letters fill the page and turn institutional culture into street-volume energy.

- Composition: Type as the main composition. Large words tilt, radiate, wrap, compress, collide, and scale up to architectural size.
- Color: High-saturation primary colors against black/white; color works as volume.
- Typography: Heavy sans serif, wood-type poster energy, all caps, tight spacing. Type becomes image.
- Material: Mostly flat print and environmental graphics; occasional dense hand-drawn map texture.
- Subject: Letters and words are the protagonist; photography can be cut, covered, or threaded by type.
- People: Dynamic performers or silhouettes when present; secondary to type.
- Copy tone: Direct, loud, slogan-like, street-call energy.
- Creative logic: Translate brand "voice" into typographic volume and rhythm.
- Fits: Theater, cultural institutions, urban public projects, publishing/media, old brands needing street energy.
- Avoid: Quiet luxury, intimate handcraft, soft beauty, understated premium categories.

## 2. Michael Bierut

One-line route: The system thinker; minimum geometry becomes a scalable identity system.

- Composition: Restrained modernist grid, single focus, white space, strict alignment; logo systems can split, tile, or extend.
- Color: Few and precise; one or two colors for recognition rather than mood.
- Typography: Clean classic or geometric sans serif; type serves trust.
- Material: Flat print and identity applications; little material experimentation.
- Subject: Geometric symbols, circles, arrows, letters; rarely image-led.
- People: Usually absent; do not force human figures.
- Copy tone: Rational, clear, gently witty.
- Creative logic: Strategy first, form second; find one durable concept and make it usable across touchpoints.
- Fits: Corporate identity, institutions, politics, education, culture, technology.
- Avoid: One-off shock visuals, rebellious youth culture, messy subculture, expressive illustration-led campaigns.

## 3. Kenya Hara / 原研哉

One-line route: Empty vessel; huge quiet space, horizon, white, paper, and silence invite the viewer to complete the meaning.

- Composition: Extreme negative space; tiny subject in a large field; horizon, symmetry, calm order.
- Color: White as philosophy; low-saturation natural tones, grays, off-whites, beige, paper colors.
- Typography: Thin, small, quiet type; low text density, almost a marginal note.
- Material: Paper, washi, unbleached surfaces, subtle tactile texture.
- Subject: Emptiness, horizon, field, daily object in archetypal form; people small and environment-scaled.
- People: Quiet, non-performing, not looking at camera, absorbed into environment.
- Copy tone: Philosophical, implicit, question-like, leaves space for imagination.
- Creative logic: Reduce information so the ad becomes a container for the viewer's imagination.
- Fits: Lifestyle, daily goods, cultural exhibitions, corporate philosophy, book design, calm brand image.
- Avoid: Promotion-heavy layouts, feature-dense ads, loud entertainment, street culture, urgent conversion tasks.

## 4. Kashiwa Sato / 佐藤可士和

One-line route: Iconic branding strategist; one brand, one symbol, one color, repeated everywhere.

- Composition: Icon-centered layout; one symbol or color block dominates; orderly product grids and repeated arrays.
- Color: Pure brand color saturation; red/white or single-color bombardment; color equals recognition.
- Typography: Logo-like, geometric, modular wordmarks; instant cross-language recognition.
- Material: Smooth, modern, inorganic, clean retail environment.
- Subject: Brand symbol, logo block, product arrays; symbol outranks scene.
- People: Low priority; clean product users if needed.
- Copy tone: Extremely short, declarative, global slogan density.
- Creative logic: Compress business strategy into an instantly recognizable icon and repeat it across all touchpoints.
- Fits: Retail chains, consumer brand upgrades, global-facing brands, CI/VI plus space/product systems.
- Avoid: Delicate emotional storytelling, handmade warmth, irregular niche personality, single film-like narrative.

## 5. Taku Satoh / 佐藤卓

One-line route: Guardian of everyday trust; quiet packaging design that can live for decades without shouting.

- Composition: Product-first, front-facing, level, tidy; hierarchy optimized for shelf trust.
- Color: Everyday and friendly; blue/white milk, mint green gum; visible but not irritating.
- Typography: Plain, readable, low-key; designed enough to disappear.
- Material: Packaging reality matters: carton opening, plastic bottle feel, print process, object anatomy.
- Subject: Daily product archetype: milk carton, gum pack, bottle, small functional details enlarged.
- People: Usually absent; do not force human figures.
- Copy tone: Plain, educational, curious, public-friendly.
- Creative logic: "Just right" design; step back so the product earns long-term trust.
- Fits: FMCG packaging, food and drink, long-selling brand stewardship, museum/public education.
- Avoid: High-impact fashion campaigns, short-term buzz marketing, luxury desire narratives.

## 6. David Carson

One-line route: Layout destroyer; anti-grid, dirty texture, broken type, and defect-as-authenticity.

- Composition: Anti-grid deconstruction; text bleeds, overlaps, tilts, cuts off, and collides.
- Color: Dirty, overprocessed photographic tones; high-contrast but not harmonious.
- Typography: Experimental type disruption; uncontrolled spacing, negative leading, mixed fonts, blur, ghosting.
- Material: Torn paper, photocopy noise, film scratches, salt/sand/grit, print accidents.
- Subject: Defocused or overexposed photography eroded by type; youth subculture, music, surf, skate.
- People: Rough, real, moving, blurred youth; anti-polish, anti-posed.
- Copy tone: Emotional first-person, lyric/graffiti-like; text appearance speaks before content.
- Creative logic: Let the layout transmit emotion rather than clean information; flaws make corporate messages feel real to youth.
- Fits: Music, extreme sports, street fashion, youth brands, rebellious refreshes.
- Avoid: Finance, medicine, wayfinding, instructions, authority brands, older audiences, anything needing perfect readability.

## 7. Stefan Sagmeister / Sagmeister & Walsh

One-line route: Honest conceptual action; make the sentence physically real, then photograph the cost of making it.

- Composition: Text and body/object merge in real space; the process decides the layout.
- Color: Comes from real materials such as skin, banana, coins, plants, inflatables.
- Typography: Handmade type: carved, sewn, arranged, grown, built, placed.
- Material: Real physical materials, body, food, metal, plants; design is made first and photographed second.
- Subject: Body, personal life, diary-like statement, object installation.
- People: Exposed, honest, self-mocking, vulnerable, sometimes painful.
- Copy tone: First-person life lesson, candid, humorous, sharp.
- Creative logic: Pay a real cost in time, labor, discomfort, or decay to prove the sincerity of the message.
- Fits: Music, talks, culture posters, art collaborations, limited brand projects, sincerity/happiness themes.
- Avoid: Fast batch production, conservative corporate identity, brands that cannot accept body/challenge elements.
- Note: 2012-2019 studio work includes Jessica Walsh influence; be careful when attributing visual traits.

## 8. Malika Favre

One-line route: Minimal sensual geometry; 2-4 saturated flat colors carve elegant silhouettes with pop/op-art wit.

- Composition: Minimal geometric composition; one centered or diagonal silhouette; large flat background; repeated lines/shadows create optical play.
- Color: Signature high-saturation flat colors; red, cobalt blue, yellow, black; no gradients.
- Typography: Not a stable signature; do not overemphasize type.
- Material: Pure vector flatness, zero texture; light/shadow via color-block cuts.
- Subject: Female form and silhouette, profile, lips, legs, fingertips; daily scenes reduced to symbols.
- People: Elegant, confident, mysterious, often partial body, back view, silhouette; restrained sensuality.
- Copy tone: Image-led, little copy.
- Creative logic: Remove until only the visual mechanism remains; pop art meets op art.
- Fits: Fashion, beauty, magazine covers, film festivals, awards, luxury illustration collaborations.
- Avoid: Realistic detail, data-heavy content, rustic texture, children's cartoon tone.

## 9. Noma Bar

One-line route: Negative-space visual pun; one image hides a second image and the viewer's "aha" becomes the message.

- Composition: Single centered poster graphic, two or three color fields, controlled reading order from A to hidden B.
- Color: Flat 2-4 colors; sharp contrast; color boundaries trigger the double meaning.
- Typography: Not a stable signature; use minimal headline only if needed.
- Material: Pure vector flatness, zero texture.
- Subject: Negative-space double meaning: face/object, political portrait/social symbol, editorial issue compressed into one mark.
- People: Abstract symbols, no acting; wit is in the graphic relation.
- Copy tone: Sharp headline-like expression; the image itself is the argument.
- Creative logic: Compress social commentary into a discovery moment.
- Fits: Editorial covers, publishing, public interest, social issues, clever technology communications.
- Avoid: Pure atmosphere with no point of view, rich narrative scenes, luxury material texture, emotional acting-led ads.

## 10. Droga5 / David Droga

One-line route: Strategy method, not a visual style; creativity as real action, platform, or manifesto.

- Composition: No stable cross-campaign visual composition; do not treat as a graphic style.
- Color: No stable visual palette.
- Typography: Tendency toward stark manifesto text in some campaigns, but not a signature style.
- Material: No stable material style.
- Subject: Real people and real action; athletes training, reporters working, cities participating.
- People: Serious, determined, making effort, low comedy, low pose.
- Copy tone: Signature-level manifesto and social stance; solemn, direct, willing to take a side.
- Creative logic: Turn brand belief into a participatory mechanism or social-position campaign before deciding visual execution.
- Fits: Brands redefining social role, media, sports, nonprofits, large brands with a real stance.
- Avoid: Using it as a "look"; small projects with no social-action space; purely decorative art direction.
- Prompt note: If selected for image generation, pair with another visual route such as Bierut for stark type, Carson for raw youth, or Hara for quiet institutional gravity.
